## What is the f-Factor?

The **f-factor** (general factor of flourishing) is a latent dimension that captures what all measures of human wellbeing have in common. Just as the g-factor in intelligence represents general cognitive ability, f represents a person's overall capacity to flourish across multiple life domains.

<Info>
The f-factor explains **71-86% of variance** in wellbeing measures according to empirical studies, making it one of the most dominant psychological factors ever documented.
</Info>

## The Positive Manifold

The f-factor emerges from a fundamental empirical observation called the **positive manifold**:

> All dimensions of human wellbeing correlate positively with each other.

This means:
- People high in positive emotion tend to also be high in life satisfaction
- Those with strong relationships also tend to have a sense of purpose
- High engagement correlates with accomplishment
- Health correlates with all psychological wellbeing dimensions

### Ecological vs Individual-Level Correlations

<Warning>
**Critical distinction**: The positive manifold exists at both levels, but they tell different stories.
</Warning>

**Ecological (country-level) correlations**  
When comparing countries, the positive manifold is _inflated_ due to aggregation:
- Countries where citizens are happier also tend to have longer life expectancy, more social support, higher GDP
- Aggregation removes within-group variance, strengthening apparent correlations
- Scripts `01_download_wellbeing_data.py` and `02_analyze_wellbeing_f.py` analyze this level

**Individual-level correlations**  
Within a single population, the manifold is _attenuated_ but still present:
- People scoring high on one wellbeing dimension tend to score higher on others
- This is the level where mutualism dynamics actually operate
- Script `04_individual_level_f_analysis.py` analyzes MIDUS 2 data at this level

```python
# From 02_analyze_wellbeing_f.py - checking the positive manifold
off_diag = corr.values[np.tril_indices_from(corr.values, k=-1)]
n_positive = (off_diag > 0).sum()
print(f"Positive:  {n_positive} ({100*n_positive/len(off_diag):.0f}%)")
print(f"Mean r:    {off_diag.mean():.3f}")
if n_positive == len(off_diag):
    print("✓ Full positive manifold confirmed.")
```

## How f is Measured

### 1. Principal Component Analysis (PCA)

The simplest extraction method treats f as **PC1** (first principal component):

- Standardize all wellbeing dimensions
- Run PCA on the correlation matrix
- PC1 captures the shared variance = f proxy
- PC1 loadings show how strongly each dimension reflects f

```python
# From 02_analyze_wellbeing_f.py
pca = PCA()
scores = pca.fit_transform(X_scaled)
loadings = pca.components_.T        # shape (n_features, n_components)
eigenvalues = pca.explained_variance_
var_ratio = pca.explained_variance_ratio_

# PC1 is the f proxy
f_scores = scores[:, 0]
print(f"PC1 (f) accounts for {var_ratio[0]*100:.1f}% of total variance")
```

**Sign correction**: PCA eigenvectors have arbitrary sign. The scripts anchor f to point in the "more flourishing" direction by ensuring the Meaning/Life Satisfaction dimension has a positive loading.

### 2. Bifactor CFA (Confirmatory Factor Analysis)

A more rigorous approach models f explicitly:

$$
y_{ij} = \mu_j + \lambda_j^f \cdot f_i + \lambda_j^{s_k} \cdot s_{ki} + \varepsilon_{ij}
$$

Where:
- $f_i$ = general flourishing factor (f)
- $s_{ki}$ = domain-specific factors (hedonic, eudaimonic, social-physical)
- $\lambda_j^f$ = loading of dimension j on the general factor
- All factors are orthogonal by construction

Implemented in `03_models_full_f.py:130-289` (Model 1).

## The omega_h Statistic

**Omega hierarchical** ($\omega_h$) quantifies how much of the _reliable_ variance in wellbeing measures is due to the general factor f:

$$
\omega_h = \frac{(\sum \lambda_j^f)^2}{(\sum \lambda_j^f)^2 + \sum_k (\sum_{j \in k} \lambda_j^{s_k})^2 + \sum \theta_j}
$$

- Numerator: variance from general factor
- Denominator: total variance (general + specific + error)
- Empirical range: **0.71 - 0.86** (Flourishing paper)

<Note>
$\omega_h$ is always ≤ $\omega_t$ (total reliability). A high $\omega_h$ means the general factor dominates, making subscale scores nearly redundant.
</Note>

At the country level (ecological data), $\omega_h$ analogue is approximated by PC1 variance ratio:

```python
# From 02_analyze_wellbeing_f.py
omega_h_analogue = var_ratio[0]  # PC1 var / total var
print(f"omega_h analogue: {var_ratio[0]:.3f}")
print(f"Paper's empirical range: 0.71 – 0.86")
print(f"Note: country-level aggregate data tends to inflate this.")
```

## Why Does f Exist?

Two competing theoretical explanations:

### Latent Variable Model (Traditional)
f is a _real causal entity_ that influences all wellbeing dimensions from "above":
- Common cause structure
- Interventions targeting f should lift all domains
- Standard psychometric interpretation

### Mutualism Model (Network/Dynamical)
f is an _emergent statistical pattern_ arising from mutual reinforcement:
- No single latent cause
- Wellbeing domains support each other through positive feedback loops
- f is real but not reified - it's the _pattern_ of coupling strength
- See [Mathematical Models](/concepts/mathematical-models) for the dynamical system implementation

<Info>
**Key insight**: Both models predict the same positive manifold, but make different predictions about:
- Intervention effects (domain-specific vs cascade effects)
- Temporal dynamics (static vs adaptive)
- Individual differences (trait vs state × dynamics)
</Info>

## Extracting f from Your Data

To compute f-scores from your own wellbeing data:

1. **Standardize** all dimensions (z-score)
2. **Check positive manifold**: verify all pairwise correlations > 0
3. **Run PCA**: extract PC1 as f proxy
4. **Sign-correct**: ensure higher scores = more flourishing
5. **Bootstrap CIs**: quantify uncertainty in loadings (n=1,000 recommended)
