## Overview

The Flourishing project implements **four complementary mathematical models** from the paper "Toward f: A General Factor of Human Flourishing." Each model addresses a different question about the nature and dynamics of wellbeing.

<Info>
**Why four models?** No single framework captures all aspects of flourishing. These models span:
- **Static structure** (Model 1: bifactor CFA)
- **Generative dynamics** (Model 2: mutualism)
- **Stability & resilience** (Model 3: Lyapunov analysis)
- **Normative thresholds** (Model 4: capabilities)
</Info>

Full implementation: `03_models_full_f.py` (1,300+ lines)

## Model 1: Bifactor CFA

### Purpose
Estimate the **strength of the general factor (f)** using confirmatory factor analysis with orthogonal specific factors.

### Mathematical Specification

$$
y_{ij} = \mu_j + \lambda_j^f \cdot f_i + \lambda_j^{s_k} \cdot s_{ki} + \varepsilon_{ij}
$$

Where:
- $y_{ij}$ = observed score for person $i$ on dimension $j$
- $f_i$ = general flourishing factor (f) for person $i$
- $s_{ki}$ = specific factor $k$ (hedonic, eudaimonic, social-physical)
- $\lambda_j^f$ = loading of dimension $j$ on general factor
- $\lambda_j^{s_k}$ = loading of dimension $j$ on its specific factor
- $\varepsilon_{ij}$ = unique variance (measurement error)

**Constraints**:
- All factors orthogonal: $\text{Cov}(f, s_k) = 0$, $\text{Cov}(s_k, s_l) = 0$ for $k \neq l$
- Each dimension loads on exactly one specific factor (bifactor structure)

### Omega Hierarchical (ω_h)

The key output is **omega hierarchical**, which quantifies f's dominance:

$$
\omega_h = \frac{(\sum_j \lambda_j^f)^2}{(\sum_j \lambda_j^f)^2 + \sum_k (\sum_{j \in k} \lambda_j^{s_k})^2 + \sum_j \theta_j}
$$

- **Numerator**: Variance explained by general factor
- **Denominator**: Total reliable variance (general + specific + unique)
- **Empirical range**: 0.71 - 0.86 (paper findings)
- **Interpretation**: If ω_h = 0.80, then 80% of reliable variance is general; subscale scores are nearly redundant

### Maximum Likelihood Estimation

Fitting function minimized:

$$
F_{ML} = \log|\Sigma(\theta)| + \text{tr}(S \cdot \Sigma(\theta)^{-1}) - \log|S| - p
$$

Where $S$ is the sample covariance matrix, $\Sigma(\theta)$ is the model-implied covariance.

- Multivariate normality (ML estimation)
- Large sample size (N > 200 recommended)

## Model 2: Mutualism Dynamical System

### Purpose
Show that a **positive manifold can emerge without a latent variable** through mutual reinforcement between wellbeing domains.

<Note>
This is the **generative model** - it explains _why_ f exists rather than just describing it. The mutualism hypothesis is the core theoretical contribution of the Flourishing framework.
</Note>

### Coupled Logistic Growth Equations

$$
\frac{dx_k}{dt} = a_k \cdot x_k \cdot \left(1 - \frac{x_k}{C_k} + \sum_{l \neq k} \frac{M_{kl} \cdot x_l}{C_k}\right) + \sigma_k \cdot \xi_k(t)
$$

Where:
- $x_k(t)$ = level of wellbeing domain $k$ at time $t$
- $a_k$ = intrinsic growth rate (how fast domain recovers when below capacity)
- $C_k$ = carrying capacity (baseline equilibrium without mutualistic support)
- $M_{kl}$ = mutualistic coupling strength (how much domain $l$ supports domain $k$)
- $\sigma_k$ = noise amplitude
- $\xi_k(t)$ = white noise process

**Key insight**: The mutualism term $\sum_{l \neq k} M_{kl} x_l / C_k$ means each domain's growth is _enhanced_ by the current levels of other domains.

### Equilibrium Analysis

At equilibrium ($\frac{dx_k}{dt} = 0$ for all $k$), the deterministic system satisfies:

$$
1 - \frac{x_k^*}{C_k} + \sum_{l \neq k} \frac{M_{kl} x_l^*}{C_k} = 0
$$

Rearranging:

$$
x_k^* = C_k + \sum_{l \neq k} M_{kl} x_l^*
$$

**Matrix form**: $(I - \tilde{M}) x^* = C$, where $\tilde{M}_{kl} = M_{kl}$ for $k \neq l$, $\tilde{M}_{kk} = 0$.

**Solution**: $x^* = (I - \tilde{M})^{-1} C$

<Warning>
**Critical property**: $x^* > C$ for all domains when mutualism is strong enough. The equilibrium is _lifted above_ the carrying capacity by mutual support. This is the mechanism by which interventions in one domain cascade to others.
</Warning>

### Calibration from Data

From `03_models_full_f.py:503-542`:

```python
def calibrate_mutualism_from_data(X_raw, cols):
    """
    Calibrate mutualism parameters from observed country-level data.
    
    Carrying capacities C_k ← column means (baseline without mutual support)
    Coupling M_kl ← scaled partial correlations (proxy for direct interactions)
    Growth rates a_k ← set to 0.5 (not identifiable from cross-sectional data)
    """
    K = len(cols)
    means = X_raw.mean(axis=0)
    
    # C slightly below observed means (mutualism lifts equilibrium)
    C = means * 0.7
    
    # Coupling from partial correlations (control for other domains)
    R = np.corrcoef(X_std.T)
    P = inv(R)  # precision matrix
    D = np.sqrt(np.diag(P))
    partial_corr = -P / np.outer(D, D)
    
    # Scale to positive coupling strengths [0.05, 0.3]
    M = np.abs(partial_corr)
    M = 0.05 + 0.25 * (M - M.min()) / (M.max() - M.min())
    np.fill_diagonal(M, 0.0)
    
    return MutualismSystem(K, a=0.5*np.ones(K), C=C, M=M)
```

### Emergent Positive Manifold

The mutualism model generates a positive manifold through **parameter variation**:

1. Simulate a population of individuals with slightly different $(C, a, M)$
2. Each individual reaches a unique equilibrium $x^*_i$
3. Across the population, $\text{Cov}(x_k^*, x_l^*) > 0$ for all pairs
4. PCA on population equilibria yields a dominant PC1 (= f proxy)

<Info>
**No latent variable needed!** The positive manifold emerges from:
- Variation in carrying capacities ("environmental" differences)
- Mutual reinforcement dynamics (coupling)
- Equilibrium lifting ($x^* > C$)
</Info>

## Model 3: Lyapunov Stability & Resilience

### Purpose
Quantify the **stability and resilience** of flourishing equilibria using dynamical systems theory.

### Jacobian at Equilibrium

The **Jacobian matrix** $J$ captures how the system responds to small perturbations:

$$
J_{kl} = \frac{\partial \dot{x}_k}{\partial x_l} \bigg|_{x^*}
$$

**Diagonal elements** (self-regulation):

$$
J_{kk} = a_k \left(1 - \frac{2x_k^*}{C_k} + \sum_{l \neq k} \frac{M_{kl} x_l^*}{C_k}\right)
$$

**Off-diagonal elements** (cross-coupling):

$$
J_{kl} = \frac{a_k x_k^* M_{kl}}{C_k} \quad (k \neq l)
$$

Implementation: `03_models_full_f.py:406-430`

### Stability Criterion

Equilibrium $x^*$ is **locally asymptotically stable** if and only if:

$$
\text{Re}(\lambda_i) < 0 \quad \forall i
$$

where $\lambda_i$ are eigenvalues of $J$.

**Dominant eigenvalue** $\lambda_1$ (largest real part) determines recovery dynamics:

$$
\tau = \frac{1}{|\text{Re}(\lambda_1)|} \quad \text{(return time)}
$$

- Small $|\lambda_1|$ → slow recovery (fragile system)
- Large $|\lambda_1|$ → fast recovery (resilient system)

### Basin of Attraction

The **Lyapunov equation** defines an ellipsoidal basin:

$$
J^T P + P J = -Q
$$

Where:
- $Q = I$ (positive definite matrix)
- $P$ solution defines the basin: $\{\delta x : \delta x^T P \delta x \leq c\}$

**Resilience** $R(x^*)$ is the size of the basin:

$$
R(x^*) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\lambda_{\max}(P)}}
$$

Larger $R$ → system can withstand larger perturbations before shifting to a different equilibrium.

Implementation: `03_models_full_f.py:640-691`

### Country-Level Resilience Scores

For each country $i$ with observed wellbeing vector $x_i$:

1. Back-solve for carrying capacity: $C_i = (I - \tilde{M}) x_i$
2. Construct country-specific system with $C_i$, shared $M$, $a$
3. Compute Jacobian $J_i$ at $x_i$
4. Extract resilience metrics: $\lambda_1$, $\tau$, $R(x_i)$

<Info>
**Predicted relationship**: Countries with higher f-scores should show:
- More negative $\lambda_1$ (faster recovery)
- Larger $R(x^*)$ (deeper basin)
- Higher mean coupling $\bar{M}$ (more mutual support)

These predictions are testable with longitudinal data.
</Info>

Implementation: `03_models_full_f.py:694-742`

<Accordion title="When to use Model 3">
**Use Lyapunov stability analysis when you need to**:
- Assess whether observed wellbeing states are **stable equilibria** or transient
- Quantify **recovery time** after shocks (recession, pandemic, life events)
- Identify **vulnerable populations** (low resilience scores)
- Test predictions about f → resilience relationship

**Assumptions**:
- System is in or near equilibrium at time of measurement
- Linear approximation valid for small perturbations (local analysis)
- Jacobian computed at true equilibrium (not measurement noise)
- Same coupling matrix $M$ applies across individuals/countries (strong assumption!)
</Accordion>

## Model 4: Capabilities-Adjusted Flourishing

### Purpose
Incorporate **Nussbaum's capability approach** - flourishing requires not just high average wellbeing but also meeting minimum thresholds in _all_ domains.

### Mathematical Specification

$$
f_{\text{cap}} = f(x^*) \cdot \mathbb{1}[x_k^* \geq z_k \ \forall k] \cdot h(Q_i)
$$

Where:
- $f(x^*)$ = standard psychometric f-score (PC1)
- $\mathbb{1}[\cdot]$ = Nussbaum threshold indicator (non-compensatory)
- $z_k$ = minimum threshold for dimension $k$
- $h(Q_i)$ = capability set size proxy (balance across domains)

**Key property**: Non-compensatory - you cannot "make up" for deprivation in one domain with excellence in another.

### Nussbaum Thresholds

From `03_models_full_f.py:950-956`:

```python
threshold_quantile = 0.25  # 25th percentile
thresholds = np.quantile(X_raw, threshold_quantile, axis=0)

# Indicator: pass if ALL dimensions ≥ threshold
above_threshold = X_raw >= thresholds[np.newaxis, :]
nussbaum_pass = above_threshold.all(axis=1).astype(float)
```

**Typical thresholds** (WHR scale, 25th percentile):
- M (Life Satisfaction): 5.0 / 10
- H (Life Expectancy): 62 years
- R (Social Support): 0.80 / 1.0
- A (Log GDP): 8.5 (≈ $5,000 per capita)

### Alkire-Foster Deprivation Index

Multidimensional poverty measure:

$$
M_0 = H \times A
$$

Where:
- $H$ = Headcount ratio (proportion identified as deprived)
- $A$ = Average intensity (mean deprivation among the deprived)

**Dual cutoff method**:

1. $g_{ij} = \mathbb{1}[x_{ij} < z_j]$ (deprived in dimension $j$?)
2. $c_i = \sum_j w_j g_{ij}$ (weighted deprivation score, equal weights $w_j = 1/K$)
3. $\rho_i = \mathbb{1}[c_i \geq k]$ (identified as deprived if score ≥ cutoff, e.g., $k = 1/3$)
4. Censored score: $c_i^* = c_i \cdot \rho_i$ (zero out non-identified)

Implementation: `03_models_full_f.py:924-1021`

### Capability Set Size Proxy

Approximate as **balance score** (inverse coefficient of variation):

$$
h_i = 1 - \frac{\text{CV}(x_i)}{\max(\text{CV})} \quad \text{where} \quad \text{CV}(x_i) = \frac{\sigma(x_i)}{\mu(x_i)}
$$

- High CV → unbalanced profile → smaller effective capability set
- Low CV → balanced profile → larger capability set
- Clip to $[0.01, 1.0]$

<Warning>
**Philosophical note**: The capability approach rejects pure aggregation. A person with near-zero Health cannot be considered "flourishing" even if other domains are excellent. Model 4 enforces this via the threshold indicator.
</Warning>

<Accordion title="When to use Model 4">
**Use capabilities-adjusted f when you need to**:
- Apply **normative standards** (not just describe empirical distribution)
- Identify individuals below minimum thresholds for policy targeting
- Assess **multidimensional poverty** in wellbeing space
- Decompose deprivation by dimension (Alkire-Foster contributions)

**Assumptions**:
- Thresholds are meaningful (theory-driven or consensus-based)
- Equal weights across dimensions (can be modified)
- All dimensions are non-compensatory (capability requirement)
- Cross-sectional data sufficient (doesn't model threshold dynamics)
</Accordion>

### When to Use Each Model

| **1. Bifactor CFA** | How strong is f? | ω_h, factor loadings | Cross-sectional, N > 200 |

### The Coupling Strength Sweep

All models integrated in the **optimal regime prediction**:

<Info>
**Core hypothesis**: There exists an optimal coupling strength $\bar{M}^*$ that jointly maximizes:
1. f dominance (PC1 variance)
2. Mean wellbeing level (equilibrium)
3. Resilience (basin depth)

Too little coupling → weak f, low wellbeing  
Optimal coupling → strong f, high wellbeing, deep basin  
Too much coupling → cascade risk (instability)
</Info>

### Model Predictions (Testable with Longitudinal Data)

1. **Bifactor → Mutualism**: Higher ω_h predicts stronger coupling $\bar{M}$ in calibrated models

## Implementation Notes

### Computational Considerations

**Model 1 (Bifactor CFA)**:
- Uses `scipy.optimize.minimize` with L-BFGS-B
- Log-parameterizes unique variances for positivity constraint
- Convergence sensitive to initialization (use PCA-based starting values)
- Runtime: ~2-5 seconds for 6 dimensions, N=100-200

**Model 2 (Mutualism)**:
- Uses `scipy.integrate.solve_ivp` with RK45 adaptive stepping
- Analytical equilibrium solution available ($(I - \tilde{M})^{-1} C$)
- Population simulation: ~1-2 seconds for 200 individuals
- Runtime bottleneck: many small ODE solves (parallelize if needed)

**Model 3 (Lyapunov)**:
- Uses `scipy.linalg.solve_continuous_lyapunov` for basin equation
- Eigenvalue computation stable (no iterative solver)
- Country-level resilience: ~5-10 seconds for 137 countries
- Watch for numerical issues if $J$ near singular (coupling too strong)

**Model 4 (Capabilities)**:
- Pure NumPy, no optimization
- Vectorized operations on entire dataset
- Runtime: < 1 second for any reasonable sample size
- Most expensive operation: quantile computation for thresholds

### Reproducibility

All scripts set `np.random.seed(42)` for:
- Bootstrap resampling (CIs on factor loadings)
- Population simulation (mutualism individual variation)
- Synthetic data generation (testing without real data)
