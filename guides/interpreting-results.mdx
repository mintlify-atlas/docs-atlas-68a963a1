This guide explains how to read and interpret the key outputs from the f-factor analysis.

## Correlation Matrix

### What to Look For

The correlation matrix tests the **positive manifold** — the foundational requirement for a general factor.

**Key metrics:**
- **Mean off-diagonal r:** Average correlation across all pairs (expect 0.50-0.80 for country data)
- **Minimum r:** Weakest correlation (should be > 0)
- **All positive:** ✓ confirms positive manifold

<Accordion title="Example: Reading the Correlation Matrix">

```
Off-diagonal correlations: 15 pairs
Positive:  15 (100%)
Mean r:    0.723
Min r:     0.542  (E_engagement_autonomy ↔ P_positive_emotion)
Max r:     0.891  (M_meaning_life_sat ↔ H_health)
✓ Full positive manifold confirmed.
```

**Interpretation:**
- ✓ All 15 pairwise correlations are positive → positive manifold confirmed
- Mean r = 0.723 is strong (for country-level data, 0.60-0.80 is typical)
- Weakest pair is Engagement-Emotion (0.542) → these are most distinct
- Strongest pair is Meaning-Health (0.891) → near-perfect alignment

**Implications:**
- Countries high on one wellbeing dimension tend to be high on all others
- This structure is **necessary but not sufficient** for a general factor
- PCA will confirm whether correlations are explained by a single dominant component

</Accordion>

<Accordion title="What if Some Correlations Are Negative?">

**⚠ Warning sign.** Possible causes:

1. **Variable coding error:** Check that all variables point in the same direction (higher = better)
   - Example: "Perceptions of corruption" should be *reversed* (lower = better)
   
2. **Wrong variable selection:** A measure might not belong to the flourishing construct
   - Example: GDP alone may not correlate with positive affect in rich countries

3. **Outliers:** A few extreme countries can flip correlations
   - Check: Sort data by each dimension, identify countries > 3 SD from mean

**Action:** Re-examine variable mappings in `01_download_wellbeing_data.py`.

</Accordion>

## PC1 Variance Explained

### What It Means

**PC1 variance = proportion of total variance explained by the first principal component.**

This is the empirical strength of the general factor f.

<CodeGroup>
```text Typical Ranges
Individual-level:  45-60%  (e.g., MIDUS Ryff subscales)
Country-level:     65-85%  (e.g., WHR aggregated data)
Item-level:        30-45%  (e.g., 42-item Ryff raw items)
```
</CodeGroup>

### Interpretation Guidelines

| PC1 Variance | Interpretation |
|--------------|----------------|
| < 40% | Weak general factor. Domain-specific factors may dominate. |
| 40-60% | Moderate f. Typical for **individual-level** wellbeing data. |
| 60-80% | Strong f. Typical for **country-level** aggregated data. |
| > 80% | Very strong f, or **ecological inflation** (aggregation artifact). |

<Warning>
**Country-level data inflates correlations** through ecological aggregation. A country average removes within-country variance, making dimensions appear more correlated than they are for individuals.

This is **Simpson's paradox** in action. Always interpret country-level PC1 variance with this caveat.
</Warning>

### Example Output

```
PC1 (f) accounts for 68.3% of total variance
PC1 eigenvalue: 4.097  (Kaiser criterion > 1: ✓)
```

**Interpretation:**
- **68.3%:** Strong general factor at country level
- **Eigenvalue = 4.097:** Dominant (Kaiser rule: keep components with λ > 1)
- If eigenvalue > number of variables / 2, f is the dominant organizing principle

<Accordion title="Why Does Individual-Level f Have Lower Variance?">

**Within-person noise:**
- People vary in life circumstances, measurement error, response styles
- A person can be high on Relationships but low on Health due to illness
- Random life events create short-term fluctuations

**Country aggregates smooth this out:**
- Averaging 1000+ people per country cancels individual noise
- Only systematic cross-country differences remain
- This creates artificially high correlations

**Implication:** Individual-level f (50-60% variance) is the **true** structural estimate. Country-level f (70%+) overestimates due to aggregation.

</Accordion>

## Factor Loadings

### What They Are

Loadings = **correlations between each dimension and f (PC1)**.

Range: -1 to +1 (in practice: 0.30 to 0.60 for wellbeing data)

### How to Read Loadings

<Accordion title="Example: Factor Loadings with Bootstrap CIs">

```
PC1 Loadings with 95% Bootstrap CI:
  P: Positive Emotion          0.398  [0.312, 0.472]
  E: Engagement (Autonomy)     0.405  [0.335, 0.463]
  R: Relationships             0.423  [0.368, 0.475]
  M: Meaning (Life Sat.)       0.431  [0.382, 0.477]  ← Highest
  A: Accomplishment (GDP)      0.387  [0.318, 0.448]
  H: Health                    0.420  [0.362, 0.471]
```

**Interpretation:**

1. **All loadings positive** → All dimensions contribute to f in the same direction

2. **Meaning (0.431) loads highest** → Life satisfaction is the purest f indicator
   - Makes sense: Cantril ladder directly asks about overall life evaluation
   
3. **Accomplishment (0.387) loads lowest** → GDP is the weakest f indicator
   - Makes sense: Rich countries can still have low subjective wellbeing
   
4. **Narrow CIs** → Loadings are stable (low sampling variability)
   - Width ≈ 0.10-0.15 is typical for n=100+ countries

5. **All loadings in 0.38-0.43 range** → Dimensions are roughly **equally weighted**
   - This is the signature of a **broad general factor**
   - No single dimension dominates

</Accordion>

### Communality vs Specificity

**Communality (h²):** Variance in dimension explained by f
- h² = (loading)²
- Example: M_meaning loading = 0.431 → h² = 0.186 → 18.6% of Meaning variance is f

**Specificity (1 - h²):** Unique variance not explained by f
- Example: 1 - 0.186 = 0.814 → 81.4% of Meaning is domain-specific

<Note>
High specificity is **expected and healthy**. It means each dimension has unique information beyond f. 

If h² > 0.90 for all dimensions, they're nearly identical (redundant measurement).
</Note>

## Country f-Scores

### What They Represent

**f-score = each country's projection onto PC1, z-standardized.**

- Mean = 0, SD = 1
- Positive f = above-average flourishing
- Negative f = below-average flourishing

### Ranking Interpretation

**What this means:**
- Denmark is **2.15 standard deviations above the mean** across all PERMA+H dimensions
- Not necessarily #1 on every dimension, but consistently high on all
- Nordic countries dominate due to balanced high performance (high R, low inequality)

</Accordion>

### What f-Score Differences Mean

A **1-point difference** in f (z-score) is equivalent to:
- 1 standard deviation across the composite of all 6 dimensions
- Denmark (+2.15) vs. USA (+0.83) = 1.32 SD difference
- Roughly the 90th percentile vs. 65th percentile in global distribution

<Warning>
**f-scores are relative rankings, not absolute levels.**

- f = 0 means "average" among the countries in your sample (e.g., 137 WHR countries)
- If you add more low-income countries, rich countries' f-scores will increase
- Always report: "f relative to [sample description]"
</Warning>

## Omega Hierarchical (ω_h)

### What It Is

**omega_h = proportion of total variance in items explained by the general factor.**

This is the **gold standard reliability metric** for bifactor models.

### Interpretation Benchmarks

<CodeGroup>
```text Published Ranges (Paper)
MHC-SF (14 items):           ω_h = 0.71-0.86
Ryff PWB (18-42 items):      ω_h = 0.60-0.78
Life satisfaction scales:     ω_h = 0.80-0.92
```
</CodeGroup>

| ω_h Value | Interpretation |
|-----------|----------------|
| < 0.50 | Weak general factor. Domain-specific factors dominate. |
| 0.50-0.70 | Moderate f. Domain specificity is substantial. |
| 0.70-0.85 | Strong f. Most reliable variance is general. |
| > 0.85 | Very strong f, or items are too homogeneous (redundant). |

### Example Output

```
omega_h analogue (PC1 var / total var): 0.683
Paper's empirical range: 0.71 – 0.86
Note: country-level aggregate data tends to inflate this.
```

**Interpretation:**
- ω_h = 0.683 is at the **lower bound** of paper's range
- This is **expected** because we're using 6 broad subscales, not 14-42 items
- Fewer indicators → lower omega_h (less redundancy)
- Country-level aggregation partially offsets this

<Accordion title="Why Is Omega_h Lower for Subscales Than Items?">

**Items within a subscale are highly correlated** (they're designed to measure the same thing).
- Example: Three "Positive Relations" items all ask about relationship quality
- High item-level omega_h (0.85) means f dominates individual items

**Subscales are more distinct:**
- Autonomy subscale ≠ Health subscale (different constructs)
- Lower subscale-level omega_h (0.65) means more domain-specific variance
- This is **theoretically desirable** — proves dimensions aren't redundant

**Rule of thumb:**
- **Item-level omega_h:** 0.75-0.90 (want high — confirms items measure f)
- **Subscale-level omega_h:** 0.60-0.80 (moderate is good — confirms dimensions have unique information)

</Accordion>

## Resilience Scores (Model 3)

### What Resilience Means

**Resilience = size of perturbations a system can absorb before leaving its basin of attraction.**

In wellbeing terms:
- **High resilience:** Country can withstand shocks (recession, pandemic) and return to equilibrium
- **Low resilience:** Small shocks tip country into persistent decline

### Key Metrics

<Accordion title="Return Time (τ)">

**τ = 1 / |Re(λ₁)| where λ₁ is the dominant eigenvalue of the Jacobian.**

- Units: time to recover 63% of a perturbation (half-life analog)
- Typical range: 5-20 (dimensionless time units)

**Example:**
```
Return time τ = 12.4
```

If a country suffers a shock (e.g., 10% drop in Health), it takes **12.4 time units** to recover 63% of the way back. Full recovery (99%) takes ≈5τ = 62 units.

**Interpretation by value:**
- τ < 5: Very fast recovery (resilient system)
- τ = 10-15: Moderate recovery speed
- τ > 20: Slow recovery (vulnerable system)
- τ → ∞: System is at a tipping point (neutral stability)

</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Basin Depth (R)">

**R = 1 / √λ_max(P) where P solves the Lyapunov equation J'P + PJ = -Q.**

- Measures size of perturbations system can absorb
- Typical range: 0.01-0.20

**Example:**
```
Resilience R(x*) ≈ 0.083
```

A perturbation of magnitude > 0.083 (in standardized units) may push the system out of its basin.

**Interpretation:**
- Small R → shallow basin → easily tipped
- Large R → deep basin → robust to shocks

</Accordion>

### Resilience vs f Correlation

<Accordion title="Expected Relationship">

**Theory predicts: r(f, resilience) > 0**

Higher f → stronger mutualistic coupling → deeper basin → more resilient.

**Example output:**
```
(a) f vs Resilience
r = 0.412, p = 2.3e-06
```

**Interpretation:**
- Positive correlation confirms theoretical prediction
- r = 0.412 → 17% shared variance (moderate effect)
- Countries with high f are **significantly more resilient** to shocks
- But 83% of resilience variance is independent (other factors matter)

**Practical implication:** Investing in broad wellbeing (raising f) has a side benefit of increasing systemic resilience.

</Accordion>

## When Results Indicate Problems

### Red Flags

<AccordionGroup>

<Accordion title="1. Negative correlations in the manifold">

**Diagnostic:**
```
Min r:     -0.123  ✗ some negative
```

**Action:**
1. Check variable coding (reverse-scored items)
2. Inspect outlier countries (plot scatter of the negative pair)
3. Verify data source hasn't changed column meanings

</Accordion>

<Accordion title="2. PC1 variance < 40%">

**Diagnostic:**
```
PC1 (f) accounts for 38.2% of total variance
```

**Possible causes:**
- Weak general factor (domain-specific factors dominate)
- Wrong variables selected (not all measuring flourishing)
- High measurement error

**Action:**
1. Check scree plot: Is there a clear elbow after PC1?
2. Run bifactor CFA: Are specific factors stronger than general?
3. Consider: Is f the right model for this dataset?

</Accordion>

<Accordion title="3. Omega_h < 0.50">

**Diagnostic:**
```
omega_h = 0.437
```

**Interpretation:** General factor is weak. Most reliable variance is domain-specific.

**Not necessarily bad:**
- May indicate genuinely multidimensional construct
- PERMA+H is **designed** to have distinct domains
- Low omega_h + high domain reliabilities = healthy multidimensionality

**Bad if:** Individual domain reliabilities are also low (< 0.60) → measurement error

</Accordion>

<Accordion title="4. Unstable equilibrium (Lyapunov)">

**Diagnostic:**
```
Jacobian eigenvalues: Re(λ₁) = +0.023
⚠ System unstable
```

**Meaning:** Country is near a tipping point (eigenvalue crosses zero).

**Implications:**
- Small shocks can cause runaway divergence
- Equilibrium exists but is repelling (unstable fixed point)
- System may be transitioning between regimes

**Example contexts:** Countries in crisis, rapid development transitions, regime change

</Accordion>

</AccordionGroup>

## Comparing Across Contexts

### Country-Level vs Individual-Level

| Metric | Country-Level | Individual-Level |
|--------|---------------|------------------|
| PC1 variance | 65-85% | 45-60% |
| Mean correlation | 0.70-0.85 | 0.45-0.65 |
| omega_h | 0.70-0.90 | 0.55-0.75 |

**Why the difference?** Ecological aggregation removes within-group variance, inflating correlations.

### Cross-Cultural Comparisons

<Warning>
**Measurement invariance is required** to compare f-scores across cultures.

Before comparing Country A vs Country B:
1. Check: Do factor loadings differ?
2. Test: Configural → metric → scalar invariance
3. If violated: f may not mean the same thing in both cultures

The country-level WHR analysis assumes measurement equivalence (Gallup uses standardized protocol). Individual-level cross-cultural comparison requires explicit invariance testing.
</Warning>
